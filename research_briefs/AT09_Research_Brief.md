# Flow states preserve functional binding while selectively reducing narrative self-monitoring

Expert flow states do **not** reduce ρ (binding/self-reference) in the functional sense that Conduit Monism requires for coherent experience. Instead, flow selectively suppresses **narrative self-monitoring** while preserving—and often enhancing—**functional binding** for coordinated action. The "no self" reported in flow refers specifically to diminished autobiographical reflection and inner criticism, not to the sensorimotor self-model that maintains coherent agency. This dissociation is neuroanatomically, phenomenologically, and empirically robust.

## The evidence distinguishes two types of self that flow differentially modulates

The neuroscience literature clearly separates the **narrative self** (autobiographical reflection, inner critic, temporal self-continuity) from the **minimal/functional self** (bodily agency, sensorimotor coordination, action ownership). These rely on distinct neural substrates: the narrative self operates through Default Mode Network cortical midline structures—particularly medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC) and posterior cingulate cortex (PCC)—while the minimal self depends on temporoparietal junction, inferior parietal lobule, and fronto-parietal sensorimotor networks.

A critical 2013 MEG study by Dor-Ziderman and colleagues with long-term meditators revealed that these self-types even operate through different oscillatory frequencies. Narrative self attenuation appeared as **high-gamma (60-80 Hz)** decreases in frontal/medial prefrontal regions, while minimal self attenuation showed **beta-band (13-25 Hz)** decreases in ventral mPFC, PCC, and bilateral parietal areas. This frequency-dependent dissociation provides a plausible mechanism for selective modulation—flow could suppress one self-type while preserving the other.

Parvizi's 2023 Stanford research explicitly distinguishes the "**I**" (bodily self centered on anterior precuneus) from the "**Me**" (narrative self in DMN)—calling them neuroanatomically separate "archipelagos" that continuously interact but can be independently modulated. This architecture explains flow's characteristic paradox: performers report feeling "selfless" while executing highly coordinated, agentic actions.

## Ulrich's fMRI studies confirm selective suppression, not global prefrontal shutdown

The most methodologically rigorous flow neuroimaging comes from Ulrich and colleagues' 2014 and 2016 fMRI studies using arithmetic tasks calibrated to individual skill levels. These studies directly tested—and **refined**—Dietrich's transient hypofrontality hypothesis.

Key findings from Ulrich et al. (2014, NeuroImage):
- Flow **decreased** activation in mPFC (self-referential processing) and amygdala (emotional arousal)
- Flow **increased** activation in left inferior frontal gyrus (cognitive control) and putamen (reward/automaticity)
- Subjective flow ratings correlated with these specific neural changes

The 2016 follow-up (Social Cognitive and Affective Neuroscience) extended these findings, showing DMN suppression (mPFC, PCC) alongside **increased** activation in anterior insula, bilateral inferior frontal gyri, basal ganglia, and midbrain. Critically, a Dynamic Causal Modeling analysis revealed that the **dorsal raphe nucleus actively down-regulates mPFC** during flow via serotonergic projections—the DRN's suppressive effect on mPFC strongly predicted participants' subjective flow experience (r = 0.60).

Van der Linden et al.'s 2021 review explicitly states: "They could not confirm the hypofrontality account of flow because **dorsolateral prefrontal areas were quite active during flow**. However, frontal areas related to self-reflective thinking were less active." This dissociation—executive attention preserved, self-monitoring reduced—is precisely what the "narrative vs functional self" framework predicts.

## The 2024 Drexel jazz study provides ecological validity

The most ecologically valid flow neuroimaging comes from Rosen, Oh, Chesebrough, Zhang, and Kounios (Neuropsychologia 2024), recording 64-channel EEG from **32 jazz guitarists** improvising to lead sheets. High-flow improvisations (versus low-flow) showed reduced frontal activity in executive control regions—supporting transient hypofrontality—while expert musicians in flow showed **increased** activity in auditory and visual cortices. The study confirmed that experience enables flow: more experienced musicians reported flow more frequently and showed distinct neural signatures characterized by enhanced sensory processing alongside reduced frontal monitoring.

This pattern—reduced reflective prefrontal activity combined with enhanced task-relevant sensory/motor processing—recurs across modalities. Limb and Braun's 2008 jazz piano study found extensive dorsolateral PFC deactivation (self-monitoring suppression) but focal **medial** PFC activation (self-expression), demonstrating that "prefrontal reduction" is topographically selective, not global.

## Integration and binding are preserved through subcortical restructuring

The neural efficiency literature strongly supports preserved—even enhanced—functional binding during expert automaticity. As skills become automatic, the brain **binds** individual movements into coherent "chunks" that execute as unified behavioral units. Wymbs et al. (2012, Neuron) showed the putamen specifically supports this "concatenation process," maintaining robust connectivity to motor output regions. This represents **enhanced motor-level binding**, not reduced integration.

Li and Smith's 2021 systematic review found that experts show "increased effective functional connectivity and decreased inefficient functional connectivity"—task-relevant connections are preserved and strengthened while redundant pathways are pruned. Professional racing-car drivers demonstrated smaller activation volumes but **stronger connections among task-related areas**, achieving coherence through focused efficiency rather than widespread engagement.

The cerebellum and basal ganglia maintain action coherence when prefrontal monitoring decreases:
- **Basal ganglia** (especially putamen): Support sequence concatenation and chunking
- **Cerebellum**: Maintains error correction, fine-tuning, and temporal coordination
- **Sensorimotor coupling**: Preserved motor-auditory integration in musicians, visuomotor integration in athletes

This subcortical "takeover" does not represent binding loss—it represents a **shift in the locus of integration** from effortful frontal control to more efficient procedural systems. The motor system "wants to bind as many individual motor movements into a fluid, uniform movement as it possibly can" (Wymbs). Automaticity thus **increases** motor-level binding while **decreasing** reflective monitoring.

## A notable gap: No direct PCI or φ measurements during flow exist

A significant limitation in validating flow's integration profile is that **no studies have measured Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI) or integrated information (Φ) during flow states**. PCI research focuses on consciousness levels (wakefulness vs. anesthesia/coma), while Φ remains computationally intractable for realistic brain networks.

However, indirect evidence suggests flow maintains high integration:
- EEG coherence studies describe flow as "brain operating in sync" with increased global connectivity
- Cross-frequency coupling (theta-gamma) during creative tasks indicates maintained information binding
- Complexity measures (sample entropy, Lempel-Ziv) show flow involves **restructured** rather than simply reduced complexity—lower frontal complexity but preserved global organization

The "entropic brain hypothesis" suggests flow represents an optimized configuration—neither maximally chaotic nor rigidly ordered—but this awaits direct perturbational testing.

## Counterevidence exists but does not undermine the core dissociation

Several findings challenge overly simple claims about flow preserving full integration:

**Dissociation-flow overlap**: Zadik, Bregman-Hai, and Soffer-Dudek (2022) found that two specific flow components—"transformation of time" and "merging of action and awareness"—were positively associated with dissociation and psychopathology, unlike other flow components. This prompted the authors to question flow's validity as a cohesive construct.

**EEG coherence reduction**: Soffer-Dudek et al. (2018) found decreased EEG coherence (reduced long-range frontal-occipital connectivity) during dissociative absorption, supporting the notion that absorption may represent "less integrated mental functioning."

**Dark flow**: Research on gambling (Dixon et al., 2017-2019) identified "dark flow"—absorption states with negative consequences. Problem gamblers experience flow that locks attention to slot machines, correlating with depression and problem gambling severity. Flow can become maladaptive when detached from skill development.

**Highway hypnosis**: Drivers can operate vehicles for long distances with no conscious recollection—demonstrating that automated behavior can occur with phenomenal gaps, though behavior remains coordinated.

However, these counterexamples do not undermine the core theoretical point:
- The dissociation-linked flow components (time transformation, action-awareness merging) are precisely the **phenomenological** features most associated with narrative self-suppression
- Other flow components (clear goals, challenge-skill balance) remain distinct from dissociation and linked to performance
- Highway hypnosis shows that **behavioral coordination persists** even during phenomenal gaps—functional binding is preserved even when conscious access decreases
- Wanner et al. (2006) found flow positively associated with well-being while dissociation was not—they overlap but are not identical

## Framework implications: ρ measures functional binding, not narrative self-monitoring

For the Conduit Monism framework, the evidence supports the following refinement:

**What ρ (binding/self-reference) should capture**: The functional self-model required for coherent agency—sensorimotor integration, bodily ownership, action coordination. This is preserved during flow and supported by temporo-parietal, fronto-parietal, and subcortical systems.

**What ρ should NOT conflate with**: Narrative self-monitoring, autobiographical reflection, the "inner critic." These are suppressed during flow via DMN deactivation but are not necessary for coherent phenomenal experience or coordinated action.

**What φ (integration) should capture**: Global information integration across brain networks. The evidence suggests flow maintains φ—EEG coherence increases, cross-frequency coupling persists, task-relevant connectivity strengthens. Integration is restructured, not reduced.

**The "no self" of flow** refers to reduced meta-awareness and self-evaluation (narrative self), not to the dissolution of the functional self-model that maintains coherent experience. Expert performers remain agents with unified phenomenal fields—they simply stop commenting on their own agency.

## Suggested framework refinements

Based on the evidence, the AT09 framework might benefit from:

1. **Distinguishing ρ_narrative from ρ_functional**: The neural and phenomenological evidence supports two separable components of self-reference. Flow reduces ρ_narrative while preserving ρ_functional.

2. **Specifying that φ can be maintained with restructured complexity**: Flow doesn't reduce integration—it reorganizes it, shifting from effortful cortical control to efficient subcortical binding.

3. **Acknowledging frequency-specific mechanisms**: The gamma (narrative) vs. beta (minimal self) dissociation suggests different self-aspects operate through distinct oscillatory dynamics.

4. **Incorporating the "locus of integration" concept**: Integration can shift between neural systems without being reduced. Basal ganglia and cerebellum can maintain coherent binding when prefrontal systems disengage.

5. **Noting edge cases**: Some flow components (time transformation, action-awareness merging) do correlate with dissociation, suggesting that certain phenomenological features involve genuine binding changes. The framework should accommodate these nuances.

## Methodological concerns and confidence assessment

**Confidence: HIGH** that narrative self is reduced while functional self is preserved
- Multiple converging methods (fMRI, EEG, MEG)
- Replicated across task domains (arithmetic, jazz, video games)
- Clear neural dissociation with theoretical predictions
- Phenomenological reports align with neural findings

**Confidence: MODERATE** that φ-like integration is fully preserved
- No direct PCI or Φ measurements during flow
- EEG coherence evidence is suggestive but limited
- Some counterevidence on dissociation-flow overlap

**Confidence: MODERATE-HIGH** that flow involves restructured rather than reduced complexity
- Neural efficiency literature is robust
- Motor chunking represents enhanced binding
- But complexity measures haven't been directly applied to flow

**Key remaining question**: Does reduced narrative self-monitoring constitute a form of "local" binding reduction even while "global" integration persists? The framework may need to accommodate multi-scale binding concepts.

## Conclusion: The answer to the core question

Does expert flow reduce ρ while preserving φ? **The answer is nuanced**: Flow reduces one component of ρ (narrative self-monitoring) while preserving another (functional binding for coherent action). Flow does not reduce φ (integration)—if anything, integration is maintained or enhanced through more efficient subcortical systems.

The "no self" in flow refers only to reduced narrative self-monitoring. Functional binding—the coherent sensorimotor self-model required for unified agency—is preserved. The neuroimaging evidence from Ulrich et al., the Drexel jazz study, and the neural efficiency literature converge on this conclusion. The framework should distinguish these self-components rather than treating ρ as unitary.

The phenomenology matches the neuroscience: flow feels "selfless" because the inner commentator quiets, but the performer remains a unified agent capable of integrated, purposeful action. This is selective modulation, not dissolution.

This transition raises a profound question for consciousness frameworks that equate "binding" with "conscious effort." If the expert no longer needs to explicitly bind these elements via a slow, deliberate global workspace process, does the binding—and therefore the consciousness—diminish?Research Brief AT09 crystallizes this into the "Unconscious Expert" paradox. Under the Conduit Monism v9.3 framework, the magnitude of consciousness ($D$) is a function of Integration ($\phi$) and Binding ($\rho$). The threat is that the expert's report of "no-self"—the feeling that "I am not doing this, it is just happening"—implies a value of $\rho \approx 0$. If $\rho$ approaches zero, then $D$ must approach zero, rendering the expert a "philosophical zombie"—a being that processes information and behaves intelligently but possesses no internal experience.However, this theoretical prediction is diametrically opposed to the phenomenological reality. Flow states are not states of stupor or unconsciousness; they are states of peak consciousness. They are described as "autotelic," "vivid," and "unified." The resolution of this paradox requires a granular dissection of what "binding" actually means in the human brain. We must determine if the "self" that disappears is the structural glue of consciousness or merely a superfluous layer of linguistic commentary.This report proceeds by analyzing four critical domains of evidence:Algorithmic Complexity: Does the information content of the brain drop during flow?Neural Anatomy of Self: Which specific networks are inhibited when "self" is lost?Network Topology: How does the brain rewire its connectivity during high-performance automaticity?The Limit Case: What does a true drop in binding look like (via the proxy of cessation states)?2. Complexity Metrics in Flow: $\phi$ and the Preservation of IntegrationThe most robust rebuttal to the claim that experts become "automatons" comes from information-theoretic measures of brain activity. If the expert brain were simply executing a pre-recorded macro without conscious processing, we would expect a decrease in neural complexity similar to that seen in Deep Non-REM sleep or anesthesia. In those states, the brain may remain active, but the repertoire of possible states it can enter (its "differentiation") collapses.To test this, we examine data utilizing Lempel-Ziv complexity (LZc) and the Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI). These metrics quantify the compressibility of neural signals; a random, highly complex signal is incompressible (high LZc), while a repetitive, predictable signal is highly compressible (low LZc).2.1. Lempel-Ziv Complexity (LZc) in Musical ImprovisationMusical improvisation serves as an ideal model for "expert flow." It requires the real-time generation of novel acoustic patterns, high technical motor skill, and the complete suspension of self-conscious editing. If the "Unconscious Expert" hypothesis were true, we would expect the improvising brain to show lower complexity than the brain performing a rote, memorized score, as it falls back on learned sub-routines.Recent high-density EEG studies 1 comparing "strict" (score-based) performance versus "improvised" performance reveal the exact opposite.The Complexity Surge:The primary finding is that LZc significantly increases during the improvised condition compared to the prepared condition. In one landmark study 1, the observed difference in LZc was 0.009 ($p = 0.031$), a statistically significant elevation. This increase was not an artifact of movement, as it was consistent across subjects and even observed in audience members who were deeply engaged in listening to the improvisation.Implications for $\phi$:In the context of Conduit Monism, $\phi$ represents the capacity for integrated information—the ability of the system to exist in a highly differentiated yet unified state. The rise in LZc indicates that during flow, the brain is not "simplifying" its processing; it is accessing a larger state space. The "improvisatory state of mind" is characterized by a state of "criticality"—poised between order and chaos—where the neural system is maximally sensitive and flexible.Hemispheric Lateralization:The increase in complexity is often localized to the right hemisphere.1 The left hemisphere, typically dominant in language, logic, and explicit rule-following (the "Narrative Self"), does not drive this complexity surge. Instead, the right hemisphere—associated with holistic processing, divergent thinking, and spatial awareness—takes the lead. This suggests that the "silencing" of the left-hemisphere narrator allows the right hemisphere to engage in a more complex, high-bandwidth form of processing.2.2. Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI) and the "Naked Awareness" BaselineThe Perturbational Complexity Index (PCI) is arguably the gold standard for measuring the capacity for consciousness. It involves zapping the brain with a magnetic pulse (TMS) and measuring the "echo" via EEG.Waking State: The echo is complex, spreads across the cortex, and lasts for hundreds of milliseconds (High PCI).Unconsciousness (Sleep/Anesthesia): The echo is local, simple, and dies out quickly (Low PCI).3While direct TMS-PCI measurements during high-movement athletic flow are methodologically challenging, we can look to "resting state" proxies in expert meditators who enter states of "naked awareness" or "pure presence"—states phenomenologically identical to flow in their lack of narrative self and total absorption.Findings:Studies investigating these "pure consciousness" states show that complexity measures do not collapse.5 The brain maintains a "readiness" configuration that is highly integrated. Even in the absence of specific sensory content (eyes closed, silence), the potential for integration remains maximal.This provides a critical validation: The mechanism of integration ($\phi$) operates independently of the narrative self-structure. The "lights are on" (high $\phi$), even if "nobody is home" (silenced narrative). The expert is not "checking out"; they are entering a state of higher dimensional processing that is objectively more complex than their baseline resting state.2.3. Multiscale Entropy (MSE) and Temporal ScalesFurther supporting the complexity argument is the analysis of Multiscale Entropy (MSE). Transient Hypofrontality vs. Selective DeactivationThe "Transient Hypofrontality Hypothesis" (THH), proposed by Dietrich 9, has long been the dominant model for flow, suggesting a broad downregulation of the prefrontal cortex (PFC). This model supported the idea of "reduced consciousness." However, newer, higher-resolution data refines this significantly.The Critique of Global Hypofrontality:Ulrich et al. 11 utilized perfusion fMRI to show that the downregulation is highly specific, not global. The DMN is well-established as the neural substrate for self-referential thought, autobiographical memory, "mental time travel" (worrying about the future/ruminating on the past), and the "narrative self".13The Role of the dlPFC:The dlPFC is part of the Central Executive Network (CEN), involved in working memory and the manipulation of information. Its continued activation during flow indicates that high-level cognitive control is not absent; it is simply directed entirely at the task rather than at the self.Conclusion: The "self" that disappears during flow is the narrative self supported by the mPFC/DMN.
