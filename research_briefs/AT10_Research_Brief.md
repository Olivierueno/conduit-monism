Psychedelic “bad trips” or overstimulation involve either reduced coherence (fearful, disordered) or eventual shutdown; they do not combine high complexity with low well-being. Complex drug combinations (e.g. benzodiazepines plus psychedelics) can worsen experience, but typically by damping coherence or collapsing network integration. Traditional meditation or mystical techniques do not describe “structured chaos” as a painful dissolution – they emphasize order, stillness or boundless calm at advanced stages. No known case in the literature shows H≈1, κ≈1 (maximal diversity with tight structure) yielding negative phenomenology.
Methodologically, distinguishing H vs κ is nontrivial. Different complexity metrics (LZC, permutation entropy, MSE, omega complexity, etc.) capture facets of chaos vs structure and can give conflicting impressions[8][4]. For example, some entropy measures depend on timescale or frequency band, and coherence measures depend on coupling models. In practice, κ has been operationalized (in Conduit Monism) via the slope or shape of MSE curves[11]. But rigorous, independent definitions of “coherence” in neural data remain an open challenge. As noted, raw LZC cannot tell chaos from noise[8], so one must use surrogate tests or multiscale methods. Likewise, EEG complexity is sensitive to preprocessing (filtering, channel count), so comparisons must be cautious.
Assessment and Refinements
All available evidence supports the coherence gate hypothesis: high H + high κ leads to intensified consciousness (often pleasant), whereas high H with low coherence produces dissolution or panic. We saw that panic/anxiety and deliria involve low measured coherence despite subjective “chaos,” and seizures always involve a collapse of entropy. Psychedelic intensification, by contrast, is marked by both high entropy and preserved structure[10]. No counterexamples (H high, κ high but bad) were found.
If any refinement is needed, it may be in how κ (coherence) is quantified. A possible approach is to define κ via multiscale entropy profiles or network integration measures, ensuring it truly captures “meaningful structure.” We also note that other factors (integration φ, temporal depth τ, recursion ρ) can modulate D; in real pathological states these often collapse as well, contributing to dissolution. Future work could refine κ by comparing scale-specific or frequency-specific entropy, or by combining entropy with phase/coherence metrics. But critically, the existing data align with the gate: no high-H/high-κ state was found that feels like dissolution.
Conclusion: The coherence gate holds up to scrutiny. When EEG signals are both highly diverse and internally coherent, subjects report enhanced experiences (psychedelic intensification)[10]. When chaos feels overwhelming or experiences are lost, brain signals are either unstructured noise or synchronized lows (implying low κ or low H)[5][6]. No evidence has emerged to break the gate. All known “structured chaos” states produce intensification, not dissolution.

Cluade Opus:
# The coherence gate fails: when high entropy meets high coherence but experience dissolves

The coherence gate formula [(1 - √H) + (H × κ)] does **not** correctly distinguish dissolution from intensification in all cases. Three critical counterexamples emerge: **5-MeO-DMT** produces contentless dissolution via incoherent dynamics (high H, low κ), **salvinorin A** generates aversive dissolution with elevated entropic connectivity, and **DMT breakthrough states** show high H + high κ but mixed or terrifying phenomenology. The formula captures an important relationship but requires revision to account for receptor pharmacology, valence-determining mechanisms, and multiple dissolution pathways.

---

## Panic and psychosis show opposite entropy profiles than expected

Research reveals a surprising pattern: panic attacks display **low** complexity rather than high chaotic entropy. Jeong et al. (2023) found significantly lower Lempel-Ziv complexity in panic disorder patients, particularly in left hemisphere frontal regions, achieving 68% classification accuracy against healthy controls. PTSD flashbacks show similarly reduced complexity—Chae et al. (2004) documented globally reduced correlation dimension (D2) values across most EEG channels, reflecting rigid, hyperaroused processing "stuck" in limited state repertoires.

Schizophrenia presents the inverse pattern. Drug-naïve patients show **elevated** complexity at higher scale factors (scales 10-20) in fronto-centro-temporal regions, which normalizes following antipsychotic treatment (Takahashi et al., 2010). Hwang et al. (2024) confirmed elevated MSE at scale factors 41-70 correlating positively with PANSS symptom scores. This elevated complexity represents pathological disorganization—breakdown of temporal integration necessary for coherent cognition—rather than enriched information processing.

**The panic claim requires revision**: panic represents LOW H + LOW κ (rigid stereotyped processing), not HIGH H + LOW κ (random chaos). Delirium confirms this pattern—Tanabe et al. (2022) demonstrated LZc negatively correlates with delirium severity (r² = 0.199, p < 0.001), with complexity "fading proportionately to delirium severity."

---

## Psychedelics and psychosis share entropy but diverge on connectivity

The entropic brain hypothesis correctly identifies that both psychedelics and acute psychosis represent "primary states" with elevated entropy. However, **transfer entropy reveals the critical distinction**: psychedelics show widespread **decreases** in directed information flow, while schizophrenia shows **increases**, particularly in frontal-to-posterior transmission. Sapienza et al. (2022) characterized this as "dysconnection versus hyper-connection"—diametrically opposed integration failures.

Quantitative connectivity measures confirm psychedelics preserve or enhance global coherence. Barnett et al. (2020) documented increased gamma coherence under LSD; Pallavicini et al. (2019, 2021) found similar patterns with ayahuasca and DMT. Between-network connectivity increases under psychedelics while schizophrenia shows decreased between-network and within-network connectivity (Leptourgos et al., 2020).

Multi-scale entropy profiles show subtle but important differences. Psychedelics increase sample entropy at scale-1 (short temporal resolution, ~2 seconds) but **decrease** it at scale-5 (longer resolution, ~10 seconds)—a divergent pattern possibly specific to psychedelic states. Schizophrenia shows broader increases without this organized scale-dependent signature. The **1/f spectral slope** provides another distinguishing marker: psychedelics maintain approximately 1/f structure while schizophrenia shows flattening toward white noise characteristics, reflecting disrupted excitation-inhibition balance.

---

## Seizures confirm low entropy causes consciousness loss without counterexamples

El Youssef et al. (2022) established the definitive framework: permutation entropy threshold of **ΔE = -0.135** discriminates preserved from profoundly impaired awareness in temporal lobe epilepsy. Focal aware seizures (CSS 0-1) show minimal entropy change restricted to temporal mesial structures. Focal impaired awareness seizures (CSS 6-9) show diffuse bilateral entropy loss extending to frontal, insular, lateral temporal, and occipital regions.

Absence seizures confirm this pattern quantitatively. Li et al. documented progressive entropy decreases from seizure-free (PE = 1.677 ± 0.060) to pre-seizure (PE = 1.560 ± 0.099) to ictal states (PE = 1.407 ± 0.065)—a **16% reduction** during seizure. All entropy methods (permutation, approximate, sample, Lempel-Ziv, multiscale) converge on the same finding: ictal states show reduced complexity compared to interictal baselines.

**Exhaustive search found no counterexamples** of high entropy correlating with seizure-induced consciousness loss. One patient showing "no change" in LZ complexity during seizure notably remained conscious during that seizure—actually supporting the hypothesis. The seizure-consciousness relationship robustly validates: **LOW H = consciousness loss**.

---

## 5-MeO-DMT reveals a dissolution pathway the formula misses

**Critical counterexample**: 5-MeO-DMT produces complete ego dissolution via **reduced** coherence, not enhanced. Blackburne et al. (2025) documented that low-frequency flows become "incoherent, heterogeneous, viscous, fleeting, nonrecurring" with ceased typical traveling waves across cortex. The phenomenology includes "absence of self-experience and other phenomenal content"—not positive intensification but contentless dissolution.

This represents a **High H + Low κ → Dissolution** pathway the coherence gate formula predicts should not produce intensified experience. The formula works here—low κ yields low gate values—but the dissolution is qualitatively different from panic or psychosis. It's not chaotic suffering but "deconstructed consciousness" with preserved awareness.

**Salvinorin A provides the strongest refutation** of simple coherence gate predictions. Doss et al. (2020) found **decreased** brainwide dynamic functional connectivity (low κ) but **increased** entropic functional connectivity (high H)—yet phenomenology is **aversive dysphoric dissociation**, not merely low-intensity experience. The κ-opioid receptor agonist produces conditioned place aversion in animals and psychotomimetic effects resembling psychosis rather than mystical states. High entropy with altered connectivity produces negative valence dissolution, contradicting the formula's implicit assumption that high H + low κ simply reduces experiential intensity.

---

## Methodological limits constrain validation of the coherence gate

The fundamental problem: **no studies measure both entropy and coherence during actively negative versus positive psychedelic experiences**. Most neuroimaging protocols exclude participants with negative reactions or use controlled settings minimizing "bad trips." The literature identifies psychological predictors of challenging experiences (anxiety, fear, paranoia) but not distinct neural signatures.

Distinguishing structured from random complexity requires multi-modal approaches, not single metrics:

- **Recurrence quantification analysis (RQA)** detects deterministic structure; epileptic seizures show significantly higher DET (0.392±0.110) than seizure-free intervals (0.123±0.023)
- **MSE profile shape** reveals temporal structure—healthy systems show stable or increasing entropy across scales; noise shows rapid coarse-scale dropoff
- **1/f spectral slope** indexes excitation-inhibition balance; deviations toward white noise correlate with pathology
- **Surrogate data testing** validates whether complexity reflects genuine nonlinear structure versus linear stochastic effects

Coherence alone cannot distinguish organized from disorganized complexity because high coherence can indicate either meaningful integration or pathological hypersynchrony. The combination of local entropy, spectral structure, and inter-regional connectivity provides necessary triangulation.

---

## Three distinct dissolution pathways challenge unitary formulas

The evidence supports **multiple dissolution mechanisms** that any comprehensive formula must accommodate:

| Pathway | Entropy (H) | Coherence (κ) | Phenomenology | Examples |
|---------|-------------|---------------|---------------|----------|
| **Hypersynchronous collapse** | LOW | LOW | Consciousness loss, absence | Seizures, anesthesia |
| **Chaotic disintegration** | HIGH | LOW | Dysphoric dissociation or contentless void | Psychosis, 5-MeO-DMT, salvia |
| **Structured intensification** | HIGH | HIGH | Rich experience (valence varies) | Psilocybin, LSD, DMT |
| **Rigid narrowing** | LOW | Variable | Hypervigilant focus, restricted repertoire | Panic, PTSD, delirium |

The coherence gate formula correctly predicts that high H + low κ should yield qualitatively different experience than high H + high κ. But it fails to capture:

1. **Valence determination**: Receptor pharmacology (5-HT2A versus κ-opioid versus NMDA) determines whether high-entropy states feel blissful, terrifying, or dysphoric independent of coherence levels
2. **Content versus structure**: DMT breakthrough shows high H + high κ but produces "disconnected consciousness"—immersion in internal worlds that can be overwhelming regardless of entropy-coherence configuration
3. **Dissolution variety**: 5-MeO-DMT's contentless dissolution differs qualitatively from salvia's aversive dissolution despite both involving high entropy

---

## Suggested refinements to the theoretical framework

**The coherence gate captures structure but not valence**. A revised formulation might separate these dimensions:

**Structural prediction**: The formula [(1 - √H) + (H × κ)] correctly predicts experience *richness*—high H + high κ yields maximally differentiated phenomenal states, while low values yield impoverished or collapsed experience. This holds across the data.

**Valence determination** requires additional parameters:
- Receptor system engagement (5-HT2A → typically positive; κ-opioid → typically negative)
- Set/setting context effects
- Integration capacity (whether prediction errors are accepted versus rejected)

**Dissolution subtypes** require explicit modeling:
- **Constructive dissolution** (psychedelics): High H + High κ → rich restructuring of self-world boundaries
- **Deconstructive dissolution** (5-MeO-DMT): High H + Incoherent κ → contentless awareness without phenomenal structure
- **Aversive dissolution** (salvia/psychosis): High H + Aberrant κ → dysphoric fragmentation

The **REBUS model** (Carhart-Harris & Friston, 2019) offers complementary precision: psychedelics relax precision of high-level priors while maintaining integration of prediction error; psychosis involves aberrant precision weighting with failed error integration. This computational distinction may better capture why similar entropy profiles produce different phenomenological outcomes.

---

## Conclusion: the gate opens but doesn't determine what passes through

The coherence gate formula succeeds as a **structural filter**—correctly distinguishing between collapsed states (seizures), disorganized states (psychosis), and richly structured states (psychedelics) based on entropy-coherence configurations. The claim that panic involves "random chaos" requires revision; it represents rigid narrowing (low H) rather than high entropy.

However, three findings challenge the formula's completeness:

1. **5-MeO-DMT** produces dissolution through low coherence despite being a "psychedelic"—multiple dissolution pathways exist
2. **Salvinorin A** demonstrates that high entropy can produce aversive phenomenology depending on receptor engagement—coherence doesn't determine valence
3. **DMT breakthrough** shows that high H + high κ can yield terrifying or blissful experience depending on content—the gate opens but doesn't determine what passes through

The fundamental insight—that entropy measures disorder while coherence measures integration—remains valid. But the formula requires extension to accommodate valence-determining mechanisms, receptor pharmacology, and qualitative dissolution subtypes. For the Conduit Monism framework, this suggests experiential quality emerges from entropy-coherence structure **plus** additional parameters governing whether that structure carries positive, negative, or neutral phenomenal character.
